Parallax

March 2016 - June 2016

I've always loved sidescrolling platformer games and wanted to take a shot at making a platformer with realistic physics and challenging gameplay. Thus I decided to create Parllax for the final summative of ICS4U1 (Computer Science) in Java, using libGDX game engine. Parallax was voted best project of the 2015/2016 year.

Summary

In Parallax, the maps are almost completely pitch black. The only source of light is the player themselves. The player steadily loses "light energy" as time passes on, and must reach a checkpoint before their light extinguishes. This makes gameplay extremely challenging as players have to rush to find the checkpoints while only being able to see their immediate surroundings.

BoxLife

March 2016 - June 2016

BoxLife was my first attempt at making a platformer game. It was created for the summaitve of ICS3U1 (Computer Science), and was created using processing. BoxLife was voted best computer science project of the 2014/2015 year and was awarded 100%.

Summary

BoxLife is a maze based puzzle game that is extremely challenging. You have a set number of lives to beat all 10 levels. Each level has a time-limit. Dying due to obstacles/enemies or running out of time will reset the level and take away one of your lives. How fast can you beat all 10 levels?

Trayble

September 2016 - January 2017

Summary

Students in the McMaster Engineering program are assigned a client with a specific problem that needs to be solved. Students brainstorm, design and implement a working solution to be presented to peers and teachers. Out of more than 250 groups, the top 10 projects are entered into a showcase attended by the clients themselves, as well as professors, peers, and the media. After all groups have presented their project, the client, with the help of industry professionals choose the best project which is then adapted by the client.

Our project, the trayble was entered into the showcase and awarded best presentation.

Client & Problem

Our client was the St. Peter's Residence at Chedoke, a long-term care residence. They noticed that the tables they used in their dining hall could only be adjusted to a single height, and made it difficult for residents with different wheelchair sizes to sit comfortably at the same table.

Solution

Our solution was the trayble. A cheap, lightweight and modular tray attachement that could be installed on any table and provided an easy way to adjust individual heights. Our focus was usability, we wanted all residents to be able to adjust the height without assistance from staff, in an effort to maintain their independance.
